
🏆 HackHarvard 2025 — Winning Projects Analysis
The Confirmed Winners
🥇 1st Place Overall — Albert Le (project name not publicly indexed, confirmed on his LinkedIn)
🥈 2nd Place Overall — ResQMe (Solan Homestead, Clark University) An ESP32-based mesh network disaster communications device that works entirely without cell towers or internet. Users can send messages and SOS signals with GPS coordinates through a peer-to-peer hardware network. Pure hardware hacking — no LLMs, no wrappers. Won purely on technical ambition and real-world necessity.
🥉 3rd Place Overall — Yumi (Team scrappydevs) An agentic social food network where AI agents learn your taste, blend friend group preferences, and orchestrate group dining experiences through natural language. Tech stack: SwiftUI (iOS), React/Next.js, Python/FastAPI, Supabase, Google Gemini, HuggingFace, ElevenLabs, Google Places API. 225 commits in 36 hours — they shipped hard.
Infosys Human Augmentation Track — Halo (Harpita & Harpith Pandian, Rutgers) An AR + AI iOS app that overlays step-by-step instructions on the real world to guide bystanders through medical emergencies like CPR in real time. "HALO" = Help And Life-saving AR Overlay. This was their 6th hackathon win of 2025.
Coolant Sustainability Track — CarbonCompass (ASU team) A location recommender that helps entrepreneurs pick business sites based on environmental footprint. Used React, TailwindCSS, Cloudflare Workers (serverless), and Google Gemini API for natural language processing of business descriptions.

🔍 What the Winners Have in Common — and What It Means for LotusHacks
1. They all solve a visceral, real-world problem. ResQMe (disaster comms), Halo (medical emergencies), CarbonCompass (climate entrepreneurship) — every winner had a crisp, emotionally resonant problem statement. "We built a chatbot for X" didn't win. "We built something that saves lives when the internet goes down" did. → For LotusHacks: lead with the problem, not the tech. Vietnam has rich, specific problems — healthcare access in rural areas, logistics for SMEs, language barriers in enterprise software.
2. Agentic AI beats chatbot AI. Yumi (3rd place overall) wasn't a "chat with AI" app — it was an agent that autonomously learned preferences, coordinated across a friend group, and executed a dining plan. Halo used computer vision + AR in real time. The winners used AI as an orchestration layer, not just a text interface. → For LotusHacks: build something where the AI acts on your behalf, not just responds to queries.
3. Multi-sponsor API integration wins sponsor prizes — and impresses judges. Yumi used Google Gemini + ElevenLabs + Google Places + HuggingFace in a single coherent product. It won because it showed technical breadth and strategically covered multiple judges' interests. → For LotusHacks: use OpenAI + OpenRouter + JigsawStack + AWS in your stack. Each integration earns goodwill from a different judge.
4. Hardware/offline-first stands out in a sea of web apps. ResQMe won 2nd place overall without a single line of Python or an API call to GPT. It just worked, physically, without infrastructure. It was the most memorable project in the room because everyone else was building web dashboards. → For LotusHacks: even a small hardware element (ESP32, Raspberry Pi, mobile-native sensor usage) differentiates you massively from the competition.
5. Branding and naming matters more than you think. HALO (an acronym with emotional resonance), Yumi (warm, Japanese-sounding food brand), ResQMe (immediately explains what it does). These teams thought about their product, not just their code. → For LotusHacks: spend 30 minutes on your name. A good name signals product maturity to judges.
6. This is literally the same organizing team. HackHarvard co-organizes LotusHacks 2026 alongside GenAI Fund. Travis Fischer and Minh-Phuc Tran (HRG, which HackHarvard alumni helped build) are judges. The judging DNA is the same: real demos, real impact, real shipping. → The patterns that win HackHarvard will transfer directly.

💡 The Synthesis: What to Build at LotusHacks 2026
Combining the judge profiles from earlier with the HackHarvard 2025 winning patterns, the ideal LotusHacks project looks like this:
A mobile-native, agentic AI app that solves a Vietnam-specific real-world problem, works across multiple AI providers, and can demo end-to-end in 90 seconds.
Strong archetypes to consider:
An AR health companion (like Halo, but for Vietnam's specific healthcare gaps — medication instructions in Vietnamese, elderly care guidance)
An agentic business operations tool for Vietnamese SMEs (automate supply chain comms, multilingual vendor coordination — hits the Enterprise track and appeals to Kai Yong)
A voice-first consumer app in Vietnamese (speech is Trung Huynh's entire thesis at Blaze AI — a Vietnamese-language voice assistant for a specific domain would be gold)
A mesh/offline communications tool for rural Vietnam (ResQMe-style, but applied to Vietnam's rural healthcare or disaster contexts — deeply resonant, technically impressive, no one else will do it)
